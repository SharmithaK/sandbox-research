## 1. How We Work — Read This First

Before anything else — a few things that matter more than any of the research protocols below.

**You are not your outputs.**

The work you produce here, whether it is strong or shaky, complete or full of gaps, does not say anything about who you are or what you are worth. You are here to try to reflect the truth as honestly as you can and to learn what research looks like along the way. That is the whole point. No one on this team is evaluating you as a person — not the lead researcher, not your duo partner, not whoever reviews your PR.

**You are not here to impress anyone.**

There are no performance reviews, no rankings, no internal politics. There is just the work, and whether it holds up. When you get feedback, it is about the specific point in the document — never about you. Please receive it that way.

**A no is always better than a half-hearted yes.**

If at any point the sprint is not working for you — the timing is bad, the topic is not landing, something in your life needs more of you right now — say so clearly and early. A clean no upfront is worth far more than a slow unravelling. We genuinely mean this. You shine either way.

**Rest is not optional.**

Please make sure you are still getting at least six hours of sleep on working days. If you cannot do that and keep up with the sprint, that is important information — share it rather than pushing through. We would rather adjust than have you burn out over a research report.

**If you cannot figure something out, ask.**

Being stuck is not a failure. The escalation steps in Section 2 are there to help you get unstuck at the right level. Use them.

## 2. Communication Standards

### Where things happen

- **GitHub Discussions** (`isrl-research/sandbox-research`) — all communication about the research problem, questions, debates, findings, and clarifications. This is where the thinking happens in public so everyone on the team can learn from it
- **GitHub Issues** — the task description and sub-part specifications live here
- **Pull Requests** — milestone report submissions and peer review

### When you are stuck — escalation order

Do not skip steps. Each step exists because it resolves most questions before they need to go further.

1. **Search for it.** If the answer exists in a searchable form, find it. Do not turn a Google search into a question
2. **Ask an AI.** Perplexity, Gemini, ChatGPT — use whichever you work best with
3. **Ask a second AI.** If the first explanation did not land, ask another one to explain it differently. Sometimes a different framing is all it takes
4. **Write down exactly what you do not understand.** Be specific — which line, which word, which document. Often, writing it down precisely is enough to see the gap yourself
5. **Discuss with your duo partner.** Tag them in the GitHub Discussion thread
6. **Discuss across the team.** Post in the GitHub Discussion so other duos can weigh in — they may have hit the same wall
7. **Tag `@isrl-research/research-core`.** This is the last step. When you do, include a short summary of your discussion so far and exactly where you are stuck. Do not tag without this summary — it helps us help you faster

### When you submit a new version of a document

If you are submitting an updated version of a document after receiving feedback, summarise exactly what changed in bullet points when creating the PR or posting a comment. Do not make the reviewer hunt for the changes. It could be as simple as:

- Added definition of X in Section 2
- Replaced secondary citation with primary gazette notification link in claim C.03
- Removed the sentence about Y — could not find a primary source to support it

## 3. The Boring Tuesday Test

Before you write anything — a claim, a sentence describing a regulation, a paragraph summarising a court ruling — keep this test in mind. And before you submit anything, apply it.

Read what you have written twice.

Read it once as the person who wrote the regulation or made the ruling you are describing. Would they feel their work was being framed as careless, inadequate, or obviously wrong in hindsight? If yes, rewrite.

Read it once as someone working at a food company whose label practices you are describing. Would they feel accused of deliberate evasion or bad faith? If yes, rewrite.

If either reading produces offense, the wording is biased. If either reading produces a warm feeling — a sense that you are praising them or crediting them for good behaviour — the wording is trying too hard.

The goal is neither. The goal is language so neutral and accurate that both readings produce nothing more than mild recognition: *yes, that is an accurate description of events.*

Write the boring version. The boring version is the correct version.

Apply this test to any AI-assisted draft before accepting it. AI has a consistent habit of slipping in language that sounds neutral but carries implicit judgment — words like "failed to," "attempted to address," "finally introduced." Trust your own reading over the AI's suggestions when they differ.

## 4. Primary Sources and How to Find Them

### What a primary source is

A **primary source** is the original document:

- The actual text of an FSSAI regulation, as published in the official gazette
- The original judgment from a court, from the court's own repository
- A Codex Alimentarius standard, from the FAO/WHO Codex website
- A peer-reviewed paper, cited by its DOI
- An official tariff schedule from DGFT

A **secondary source** is someone else's description of that document:

- A news article about what an FSSAI amendment says
- A blog post summarising a court ruling
- A legal database entry describing a regulation
- A Wikipedia article about a Codex standard

Secondary sources are useful for two things: getting oriented in an unfamiliar topic, and finding that a primary document exists. They are not citable in your report. If you cannot find the primary document, you cannot make the claim.

### How to actually find a primary source

Most first-time researchers hit a wall here because primary sources are rarely the first result on a Google search. Here is the process that works:

**Step 1: Use a secondary source to identify what you are looking for.**
Search the topic in plain language. A news article or blog post will usually name the specific regulation, notification, or case you need. You are not citing this — you are using it as a map to find the real thing.

**Step 2: Go to the official source directly.**

| What you need | Where to find it |
|---|---|
| FSSAI regulations and gazette notifications | [fssai.gov.in](https://www.fssai.gov.in) → Regulations → Gazette Notifications |
| Indian court judgments | [indiankanoon.org](https://indiankanoon.org) — search by case name or citation |
| Codex Alimentarius standards | [fao.org/fao-who-codexalimentarius](https://www.fao.org/fao-who-codexalimentarius) |
| DGFT and ITC-HS schedules | [dgft.gov.in](https://www.dgft.gov.in) |
| Peer-reviewed papers | [scholar.google.com](https://scholar.google.com) — follow the DOI link |

**Step 3: Find the specific location within the document.**
A primary source is only useful if you can point to the exact place where it says what you claim. Note the regulation number, clause, section, or page. If you cannot find the specific location, you do not yet have what you need.

### Court cases as sources of friction

Court cases are one of the best places to find documented real-world friction. When a company and a regulator disagree about how a rule applies, it often ends in a court or tribunal. The judgment describes what the disagreement was, what each side argued, and what was decided — which is exactly the kind of gap your sub-parts are trying to document.

For allergen-related friction, try searching:
- `site:indiankanoon.org allergen FSSAI`
- `site:indiankanoon.org food labelling declaration`
- `site:indiankanoon.org food safety standards allergen`

Do not cite a case unless you have read the actual judgment and confirmed it says what you think it says.

### When you hit a dead end

If you cannot find a primary source for a claim, do not make the claim. Write explicitly in your report: *"No primary source was found for X."* This is a valid research finding. Gaps in the documented record tell us something important about the state of the field.

## 5. Writing Standards

### Use simple words

If a sentence can be said in plain language without losing precision, say it in plain language. Technical and legal terms are appropriate when they refer to something specific that has no plain equivalent — not as a substitute for clear thinking.

| Avoid | Use instead |
|---|---|
| utilise | use |
| stakeholder ecosystem | the companies, regulators, and researchers involved |
| operationalise | apply, implement |
| granular | detailed |
| robust framework | *(describe what it actually does)* |
| paradigm shift | *(say what actually changed)* |

### Describing regulations neutrally

Describe what the document says. Do not characterise whether it is good, bad, progressive, or inadequate. If two sources disagree, report both.

Do not use language that implies intent beyond what the document states. *"The regulation requires X"* is accurate. *"The regulation attempts to address X"* implies the attempt may have failed — only say this if there is evidence it did.

When a regulation was amended, describe what changed and when. Do not frame the earlier version as a failure or the later version as a correction unless the document itself uses that language.

**Accurate:** *"The 2020 Regulations introduced mandatory source qualification for edible oils, which was not present in the 2011 version."*

**Avoid:** *"The 2011 Regulations failed to address source qualification, a gap corrected by the 2020 amendments."*

### Distinguishing what is required from what happens

For this report especially, you will find gaps between what the regulation says and what evidence shows about actual practice. These must always be clearly separated. Never imply that a gap between regulation and practice reflects intent — describe it as a condition and let the evidence speak.

## 6. AI Use

Using AI to search for sources, understand an unfamiliar term, or get oriented in a topic you have not read before is fine and encouraged. Using AI to understand what a regulation or ruling is saying before you read it yourself is fine. Using AI to proofread a draft for grammar and clarity is fine.

**What is not acceptable:** using AI to write claims you have not verified yourself, or citing a source because AI told you it exists without reading it. You are responsible for every claim in your report. If AI suggested a source, find it, read it, and decide for yourself whether it supports the claim. If you cannot find it, do not cite it.

**The practical test:** could you defend every sentence in your report in a conversation with someone who has read all the primary sources? If yes, the AI use was fine. If no, rewrite until yes.

AI has a consistent habit of replacing simple words with jargon and adding qualifiers that sound neutral but carry implicit judgment. Apply the Boring Tuesday Test to any AI-assisted draft before accepting its wording.

## 7. Submission Format

> Milestone 1 and 2 reports do not need a paper table or claims list. Just submit what the milestone asks for. The full format below applies from **Milestone 3 onward** — introduced gradually so it does not land all at once.

From Milestone 3, every report submission has three parts.

### Part A — Claims list (in the appendix of your report)

Before writing the report, write 5–6 bullet points stating what the report is actually trying to say. One or two lines per claim. If you cannot distill the report to this, the question you are trying to answer is not yet clear enough to write. Stop and clarify the question first.

Number the claims by importance: C.01 is the most important. Claim IDs follow the format `ALLER.C.01` for the allergen report.

The claims list goes in the **appendix** of your main report file — not as a separate file.

### Part B — Paper table (a separate `.papertable.md` file)

A separate file with one row per source used. Name it `SP0X-milestone-N.papertable.md`.

| S.No | Title | DOI / Link | Claim Supported | Type of Support | Relevance |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | FSSAI Labelling Regs 2020 | [direct url] | ALLER.C.01 | direct | Regulation 5(14), page 12 |

**Type of Support** is either `direct` (the source explicitly states the claim) or `inferred` (the claim follows from what the source says but is not stated directly). Both are valid — but they must be labelled correctly.

**Relevance** must be specific enough that someone else can find the exact location without reading the whole document. "Page 12, Regulation 5(14)" is useful. "See the FSSAI document" is not.

A source that appears in your report but not in the paper table is a problem. A source in the paper table that does not map to any claim is also a problem.

### Part C — The main report

Written after the claims list and paper table are complete. Every factual claim in the report corresponds to a claim in the appendix and at least one row in the paper table. The report does not display the claim IDs inline — they live in the appendix. The report should be readable as a standalone document by someone who has not read anything else about the project.

**Why this order matters:** writing the claims first forces clarity about what you are actually arguing before any prose is written. The paper table forces you to check whether the sources you have actually support those claims — before you have written paragraphs around them that are hard to revise. The report then almost writes itself, and any gap between what you want to say and what the sources support is visible before submission rather than after review.

## 8. LaTeX Basics

You do not need to know LaTeX to start. Use [Overleaf](https://www.overleaf.com), which runs in the browser — nothing to install. Here is a minimal working document — copy it into Overleaf and start typing in the English sections:

```latex
\documentclass{article}

\begin{document}

\section{Introduction}
Write your text here.

\section{Findings}
More text here.

\subsection{Sub-section}
You can nest sections like this.

\appendix
\section{Claims List}
\begin{itemize}
  \item ALLER.C.01 — Write your first claim here.
  \item ALLER.C.02 — Write your second claim here.
\end{itemize}

\begin{thebibliography}{9}

\bibitem{fssai2020}
FSSAI. \textit{Food Safety and Standards (Labelling and Display) Regulations, 2020}.
Gazette of India. Available at: https://www.fssai.gov.in/[link]

\end{thebibliography}

\end{document}
```

Cite a source in text using `\cite{fssai2020}`. That is genuinely all you need to start. You will learn the rest as you go — ask in the GitHub Discussion if something in Overleaf is not working and you cannot figure it out after searching.

## 9. Peer Review Standards

When you review another duo's sub-part in Milestone 5, you are checking whether the sources support the claims — not whether you agree with the analysis.

For each claim in the report, check:
1. Does the cited source exist at the link provided?
2. Does the source actually say what the claim says it says, at the location specified?
3. Is the type of support (direct or inferred) correctly labelled?

If something does not check out, raise it in the GitHub Discussion with:
- The specific claim (by claim ID or by quoting the line)
- The specific source
- What you found when you checked it

**Always reference the point, not the person.**

*"Claim ALLER.C.02 cites Regulation 5(14) but the provision at that location addresses format, not quantity thresholds"* is the right framing.

*"You got this wrong"* is not.

Praise can be directed at the person. Critique is always directed at the specific point.
